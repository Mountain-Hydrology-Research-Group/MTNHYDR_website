Forests and Snow Storage in the PNW

Measuring Snow Interception with FFT-Based Video Processing

Snow intercepted by forest canopies dictates, among other properties, how much snow accumulates on the surface below the canopy. Given that nearly half of the snow covered zone in the Northern Hemisphere is forested and up to two thirds of falling snow is intercepted by the canopy, understanding the magnitude and timing of interception events is important for accurately quantifying snow accumulation over the course of a season. Despite its importance to hydrologic modeling, measuring canopy snow interception is challenging. As a result, most models depend upon previous interception parameterizations representative of specific regions and climates, leading to a significant amount of uncertainty in model output.

Building upon a growing body of work relating the sway frequency of a tree to other physical properties, we are evaluating whether FFT-based video processing of tree sway can be used to infer changes in tree mass due to snow interception. Existing studies have demonstrated the potential of using accelerometers to measure tree sway frequency and quantify various tree properties (including snow interception). However, accelerometers only provide point measurements of frequency and can be cumbersome to install, which limits the spatial and temporal resolution over which measurements can be collected. Video cameras represent an inexpensive alternative that most notably improve the spatial resolution of frequency measurements. Hence, we propose spectral analysis of pixel brightness time series can be used to map sway frequency across a domain an measure snow interception. In particular, we are:

  1. Investigating the degree to which tree sway frequency measurements obtained from fft-based video processing agree with accelerometer measurements of the same region
  2. Assessing what factors (e.g. resolution, angle, meteorologic conditions) are important to consider when extending video processing techniques to trees
  3. Concluding as to whether video processing offers a robust alternative to accelerometers for measuring tree sway frequency across time and space
